Why Closing Cost Estimates Have Such Crazy Ranges

June 16, 2026 · 5 min read

Borrower Guides

If you've ever read a loan estimate and seen a fee quoted as a range — like a "loan tie-in fee" listed at "$150 to 1%" — you're not crazy to be alarmed. On a higher-priced home, 1% can be over ten thousand dollars. A range that wide isn't a real quote; it's a placeholder. Here's how to think about it.

Why the ranges are so wide

Early disclosures are often built to cover the lender legally for the maximum a fee could be, not what you'll actually pay. Some fees are genuinely percentage-based (so they scale with loan size), while others are flat but shown as a worst-case band until the file is finalized. The result: estimates that technically aren't wrong but are nearly useless for budgeting.

How to get your real number

You have more leverage here than you think:

  • Demand a line-itemized Loan Estimate, then ask the lender to explain any fee shown as a range. Make them commit to a specific figure.
  • Compare the same fee across two or three lenders. Wildly different numbers for the same service tell you which one is padding.
  • Watch the "tie-in," "processing," and "junk" fees — these are where ranges hide and where negotiation lives.
A fee you can't get explained in one sentence is a fee worth questioning. Clarity is your cheapest negotiating tool.

A realistic SoCal frame

In higher-cost markets like Southern California, your dollar closing costs are larger simply because the loan is larger — but as a percentage, they shouldn't balloon. If a lender's percentage-based fees push your total well past the normal band, that's your cue to shop. Get every range converted to a hard number before you commit to a lender, not at the signing table.

Educational only — not a quote. Ask your lender to convert every estimated range into a firm figure for your specific loan.
