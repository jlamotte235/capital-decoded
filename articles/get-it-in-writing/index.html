If It's Not in Writing, Don't Count On It

June 19, 2026 · 4 min read

Borrower Guides

Real estate runs on documents, not memories. The friendliest verbal promise — "of course we'll leave the washer and dryer," "don't worry, that fee's waived," "your rate's locked" — means nothing if it isn't written down. Adopt one rule and it'll save you more than once: if it's not in writing, it doesn't exist.

Where verbal promises go to die

  • Items that "convey" with the house (appliances, fixtures, that shed) — if it's not in the contract, the seller can take it.
  • Repairs and credits agreed to after inspection — get them documented, not just nodded to.
  • Rate locks and fee waivers from your lender — "we'll take care of that" isn't a lock; a written lock confirmation is.
  • Closing dates, possession dates, and who pays what — all of it.
Memories fade and people move on. Paper doesn't. Every promise that matters belongs in the contract or in an email you can point to.

The simple habit

When anyone tells you something important, reply in writing: "Confirming we agreed to X — please reply to confirm." Now you have a record. It feels almost rude the first few times. It isn't — it's how professionals protect themselves, and the good ones will respect you for it.

Clarity beats trust here, every time. Get it in writing, and the deal can't quietly change underneath you.

Educational only — not legal advice. When in doubt, have agreements reviewed by your agent or attorney.
