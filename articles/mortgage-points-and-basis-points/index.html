Mortgage Points & Basis Points, Decoded

June 18, 2026 · 5 min read

Borrower Guides

Two pieces of mortgage jargon trip up almost every first-time buyer: points and basis points. They sound technical. They're not. Here's the plain version.

Buying "points" (discount points)

A point is a fee equal to 1% of your loan amount, paid upfront to your lender in exchange for a lower interest rate. So on a $400,000 loan, one point costs $4,000 and might knock your rate down a fraction of a percent.

You're essentially prepaying interest to save more interest later. Whether it's worth it comes down to one question: how long will you keep the loan?

  • Stay a long time → buying points can pay off, because the monthly savings add up past what you paid upfront.
  • Sell or refinance soon → you may never reach the "break-even" point, and the upfront cost is wasted.

Always ask your lender for the break-even month — the point where your monthly savings have repaid the upfront cost.

"Basis points" (bps)

A basis point is just one-hundredth of a percent — 0.01%. So 25 basis points = 0.25%, and 100 basis points = 1%. Lenders and the market talk in bps because it's precise: "rates moved 50 bps" is cleaner than "half a percentage point."

A point = 1% of your loan (a fee). A basis point = 0.01% (a unit of rate). Different things, similar names — that's why people mix them up.

The takeaway: points are a choice, not a requirement. Run the break-even math before you pay for a lower rate — and never let the jargon pressure you into a decision you don't understand.

Educational only — not financial advice. Run the numbers for your specific loan and timeline.
