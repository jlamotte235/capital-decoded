Prepaids vs. Closing Costs — What's the Difference?

June 16, 2026 · 6 min read

Borrower Guides

When you buy a home, the cash you bring to the table beyond your down payment splits into two very different buckets: closing costs and prepaids. Most buyers blur them together, get surprised at the end, and never fully understand what they paid for. Let's fix that.

Closing costs: the cost of doing the deal

Closing costs are the one-time fees to create the loan and transfer the property. You pay them once and they're gone. Common ones:

  • Lender fees — origination/underwriting/processing charges.
  • Title insurance + escrow/settlement fees — protecting and handling the transaction.
  • Appraisal and credit report fees.
  • Recording fees and transfer taxes paid to the county/city.

As a rough planning range, closing costs often land around 2%–5% of the loan amount — but the spread is wide and depends heavily on your lender and location.

Prepaids: money you'd owe anyway, just paid early

Prepaids aren't really "fees" — they're future expenses the lender collects upfront and parks in an escrow/impound account so your taxes and insurance are always covered. This is the part that blindsides people. They include:

  • Homeowners insurance — typically the first full year paid upfront, plus a couple months into escrow.
  • Property tax reserves — and this is the big one: lenders often collect 2 to 8 months of property taxes upfront depending on where you are in the tax calendar. On a pricey home, that can be thousands of dollars by itself.
  • Prepaid interest — interest covering the days between closing and your first payment.
Prepaids aren't extra money lost — it's your own money, collected early. But you still have to have it at closing, so it absolutely belongs in your budget.

A simple way to budget

| Bucket | What it is | Rough planning range | |---|---|---| | Closing costs | One-time fees to do the deal | ~2%–5% of loan | | Prepaids | Insurance + tax + interest reserves paid upfront | Varies widely; tax reserves alone can be 2–8 months |

The honest takeaway: ask your lender for a Loan Estimate early, and have them walk you through the prepaids line by line — especially the property tax reserve, since timing in the tax year swings it the most. The number that surprises people is almost always in the prepaids column.

Educational only — your actual figures will vary; confirm them with your lender and escrow officer.
