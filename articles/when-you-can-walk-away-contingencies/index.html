When You Can Walk Away — Contingencies, Decoded

June 19, 2026 · 6 min read

Borrower Guides

One of the scariest feelings as a buyer is signing a contract and assuming you're now locked in no matter what. You're usually not. A well-written offer includes contingencies — conditions that let you renegotiate or walk away with your deposit if something goes wrong. They're your exit ramps. Know them.

The three contingencies that matter most

Inspection contingency. After your inspections, if the home has problems you didn't sign up for, this lets you ask for repairs or credits — or cancel. This is why you never skip inspections (and why a separate roof inspection is smart).

Appraisal contingency. If the lender's appraisal comes back lower than your offer, you're not forced to overpay. You can renegotiate the price, cover the gap, or walk. Without this contingency, a low appraisal can cost you real money or your deposit.

Loan / financing contingency. If your financing falls through despite a good-faith effort, this protects your deposit instead of leaving you on the hook.

Contingencies are the difference between "I changed my mind" (you lose your deposit) and "a condition wasn't met" (you keep it). Know which exits you have before you need them.

The catch: deadlines and waivers

Contingencies have time limits. Miss the window and the protection can lapse. And in hot markets, buyers sometimes waive contingencies to win a bid — which can be a powerful offer move but removes your safety net. Never waive one without understanding exactly what you're giving up.

The takeaway: read your contract for what's contingent and by when. Those clauses are the legal version of a fire exit — you hope you won't need them, but you want to know where they are before there's smoke.

Educational only — not legal advice. Have your agent or attorney review your specific contingencies and deadlines.
