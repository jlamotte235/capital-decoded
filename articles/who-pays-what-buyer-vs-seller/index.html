Who Pays What — Buyer vs. Seller Costs

June 16, 2026 · 5 min read

Borrower Guides

A real estate closing isn't a one-sided bill. Both sides bring money to the table, and which side pays for what is partly custom and partly negotiable. Knowing the typical split helps you budget — and gives you something to trade in negotiations.

What the buyer usually pays

  • Loan-related costs — origination, appraisal, credit report, underwriting.
  • Most of the prepaids — homeowners insurance, property tax reserves, prepaid interest.
  • Often the lender's title policy and a share of escrow fees.
  • Inspections (home, and ideally roof — more on that elsewhere).

What the seller usually pays

  • Real estate commissions (historically the big one).
  • The owner's title insurance policy in many areas.
  • Their share of prorated property taxes up to closing.
  • Any agreed-upon repair credits or concessions.

The part nobody tells you: it's negotiable

These "who pays" customs vary by region and are frequently negotiated. In a buyer's market, sellers may cover part of your closing costs or buy down your rate to get the deal done. In a hot market, you may take on more to stand out.

"Who pays" isn't fixed law — it's a custom you can negotiate. In a slow market, ask the seller to cover costs; you'll be surprised how often it works.

The takeaway: don't just budget your own line items. Understand the seller's costs too, because every one of them is a potential lever in your offer.

Educational only — local customs vary; your agent and escrow officer can confirm what's standard in your area.
